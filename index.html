Obstetric Hemorrhage

Leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide. Master antepartum (previa, abruption) and postpartum hemorrhage (4 T's), risk factors, emergency management, and nursing care.

Overview & Types of Obstetric Hemorrhage

Obstetric hemorrhage is excessive bleeding during pregnancy, labor, or postpartum. It is a leading cause of maternal mortality worldwide, responsible for approximately 25% of all maternal deaths.
  • Antepartum hemorrhage (APH): Bleeding after 20 weeks gestation before delivery. Causes: placenta previa, placental abruption, vasa previa, uterine rupture.
  • Intrapartum hemorrhage: Bleeding during labor (e.g., abruption, uterine rupture, vasa previa).
  • Postpartum hemorrhage (PPH): Bleeding after delivery. Early PPH (first 24h) most common; Late PPH (24h – 12 weeks).

Antepartum Hemorrhage (Previa & Abruption)

🩸 Placenta Previa
• Painless, bright red bleeding
• Soft, non‑tender uterus
• Risk factors: prior previa, cesarean, multiparity, age >35
• Diagnosis: transvaginal ultrasound
• Management: pelvic rest, hospitalization if bleeding, corticosteroids, cesarean delivery
⚠️ Placental Abruption
• Painful, dark red bleeding (may be concealed)
• Tender, rigid, board‑like uterus
• Risk factors: hypertension, trauma, smoking, prior abruption
• Diagnosis: clinical; ultrasound may show clot
• Management: IV access, blood transfusion, immediate delivery
🔑 Key distinction: Previa – painless bleeding; Abruption – painful bleeding with uterine tenderness.

Postpartum Hemorrhage – The 4 T's

💪 Tone (Uterine Atony)
Most common cause (70–80%). Boggy uterus. Risk factors: overdistension, prolonged labor, multiparity, magnesium, chorioamnionitis. Management: massage, oxytocin, methylergonovine, carboprost, misoprostol.
🧩 Tissue (Retained Products)
Retained placenta or membranes. Manual removal, D&C, uterine packing.
🩹 Trauma (Lacerations, Hematoma)
Genital tract lacerations (cervical, vaginal, perineal), uterine rupture, inversion. Repair surgically, manage hematoma with ice, drainage.
🩸 Thrombin (Coagulopathy)
DIC, pre‑existing bleeding disorders. Treat underlying cause, replace clotting factors (FFP, cryoprecipitate, platelets).
🚨 PPH definition: Blood loss >500 mL after vaginal delivery or >1000 mL after cesarean, or any bleeding causing hemodynamic compromise.

Emergency Management & Interventions

  • Initial assessment: Vital signs, fundal tone, blood loss estimation (weigh pads, count clots).
  • IV access: Two large‑bore (16–18G) catheters, crystalloid resuscitation (Ringer's lactate), type and crossmatch.
  • Uterine massage – immediately if boggy.
  • Medications: Oxytocin (first‑line), methylergonovine (avoid hypertension), carboprost (avoid asthma), misoprostol (rectal).
  • Mechanical / surgical interventions: Intrauterine balloon tamponade (Bakri), uterine artery embolization, B‑Lynch suture, hysterectomy (last resort).
  • Blood product replacement: PRBCs, FFP, platelets as per massive transfusion protocol.
  • Monitoring: Vital signs q15min, urine output, oxygen saturation, coagulation studies.

Nursing Role & Prevention

  • Risk identification: Screen for risk factors (grand multiparity, prior PPH, anemia, placenta previa, abruption).
  • Active management of third stage: Oxytocin within 1 minute of birth, controlled cord traction, uterine massage.
  • Postpartum assessment: BUBBLE‑HE (Breasts, Uterus, Bladder, Bowel, Lochia, Episiotomy, Homan’s, Emotions). Fundal checks q15min first hour, then q30min, then hourly.
  • Emergency preparedness: Have uterotonics, IV supplies, and blood products available.
  • Patient education: Report heavy bleeding (>1 pad/hour), large clots, dizziness, foul discharge.
  • Emotional support: Hemorrhage is traumatic; provide reassurance, explain interventions, involve family.
